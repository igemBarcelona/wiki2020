Needs finding survey


We carried out a survey with the aim of identifying the main challenges faced by a researcher or a laboratory technician in their daily laboratory procedures and, consequently, to propose a synthetic biology approach to face those challenges.

Output: some of the annotated ideas were: improvement of the laboratory equipment performance, optimization of cloning strategies or other time-consuming strategies and the development of fully automated procedures.

Adjustment: even though some of the previous ideas were considered, we were balancing other projects with huge potential as well. Nevertheless, the identified daily laboratory challenges can be a great starting point for future iGEM team projects.

First brainstorming sessions


We went through several brainstorming sessions where we discussed local problems that affected real people and real-life situations. Needs finding is a long and profound process. During these sessions we talked with our advisors as well as other researchers in our university.

Some of the discussed topics were:

Lindane detection with a biosensor
A bioremediation approach to detect lindane insecticide levels in water so as to know if it is safe to be consumed by humans.
Non-invasive early diagnosis of melanoma
An approach contemplating the design of a microneedle array capable of detecting specific miRNA overexpressed in melanomas in the skin interstitial liquid.
Phtalates detection with a biosensor
Phtalates is a composite present in the air near industrial areas which is potentially damaging for the human body. The aim of this approach was to degrade phtalates into some innocuous molecules.

A researcher, the head of Translational Synthetic Biology laboratory in the Biomedical Research Park of Barcelona. He has been working on a variety of areas including mammalian genome editing, gene therapy and human skin microbiome engineering.

Input: He gave us some of the results of a study they did related to skin microbiome and Cutibacterium acnes bacteria. These results showed a list of some skin biomarkers, among them T3 hormone!

Output: From this, we continued investigating and came up with the idea of what our project is right now, but still there were many unsolved questions and some gaps we needed to fill. We could say that our first steps of our final project started here.

Final brainstorming session with advisors


On May 17th we had the final elections to decide the project we wanted to dedicate the whole summer to. We discussed the feasibility, advantages, disadvantages, limitations, applications, what topics would we work on and learn.

Output: We finally balanced our preferences towards Hormonic because currently there is not much research on thyroid system problems, there was a society need and because our group, formed by biomedical engineering students, wanted to develop an innovative and groundbreaking approach to solve this problem which unfortunately is quite prevalent nowadays.

Javier Macia Santamaria


The head of the laboratory of Synthetic Biology for Biomedical Applications, in the Biomedical Research Park of Barcelona. He has been currently working on the recovery of polluting waste through its transformation by various consortiums of microorganisms.

Input: He gave us the ideas of accomplishing an automated feedback system with a lactone circuit as a proof of concept.

Output: Our wetlab team focused on engineering E.Coli so that it can produce lactone and a second strain acting as biosensors to detect the amount of produced lactone.

Guillermo Nevot

Former head of department of the Endocrinology Service at Hospital Mutua Terrassa and at the General Hospital of Barcelona.

SCEN


Catalan Society of Endocrinology and Nutrition. It promotes the development of tasks of medical and scientific interest within the endocrinology, metabolism and nutrition fields. We asked them for an overall sight over the project.

Input: They stated that sweat is a variable and unstable secretion which makes it extremely challenging to relate the amount of T3 detected to a proper thyroid disease diagnosis and treatment. Moreover, they emphasized the importance of an individualized treatment because a low level of T3 is not always a consequence of hypothyroidism. However, they showed interest in our approach, especially for future application in patients suffering from malabsorption who cannot take the drug in pill form but through a weekly intramuscular puncture.

Output: The idea of detecting T3 hormone at skin level was discarded. Instead, a microneedle capable of reaching interstitial liquid (or blood) was contemplated.

ETA


European Thyroid Association. The European Thyroid Association's aims are to promote knowledge in the thyroid field (fundamental and clinical) and improve knowledge of the thyroid gland and its diseases. We carried out an interview with Eric Fliers, member of the executive committee and co-author of the European Thyroid Association (ETA) and Thyroid Federation International (TFI) Joint Position Statement on the Interchangeability of Levothyroxine Products in EU Countries article. Together with him we designed an insertion and monitoring plan for our new treatment.

Input: He stated that each tissue has its own organ-specific regulation by a deionization of T4 into T3, depending on its local enzyme concentration. Hence, we should take into consideration that we are detecting T3 locally and it does not need to involve the same levels of hormone in other tissues. He advised us to protect the levothyroxine pump from external light because otherwise it would degrade and confirmed that plasma is a reliable source to detect hormones. Regarding the content of the previously mentioned article, he gave us advice on how to fulfill their demands on bioequivalence. Overall, He strongly believed that even though the technology needed to accomplish a device like âHormonicâ is super complex, it is worth to investigate on it and give it a try. It would have a huge impact on helping the human body regulate its metabolic needs.

Output: Reinforcement of the statement made by the members of the SCEN, which affirms that the local hormonal condition of the skin cannot be extrapolated to the global condition of the patient. Together with him we designed a test to ensure bioequivalence between current treatments and Hormonic; and a subsequent monitoring plan to keep the patientâs evolution under observation.

Carles Zafon (SEEN)


SEEN: Spanish Society of Endocrinology and Nutrition

Input:He remarked the existence of a circadian rhythm of T3 and its delayed response to the infusion of levothyroxine due to levothyroxineâs (L-T4) long half-life and its slow uncharacterized metabolization into T3. He explained that analytic determinations are done via the quantification of free hormone concentration, every 6 months, but that the time at which the real effect of the dosage adjustment is unknown, and maybe it should be done earlier. On top of that, he pointed out that there are several factors such as age, other diseases and drugs that play a role on thyroid hormone levels. Finally, a list of possible patients that could benefit from the use of Hormonic was made.

Output:It was concluded that pre-clinical studies should be carried out in order to characterize the circadian rhythm that should be set as the reference value of our PID control system and another one that characterized the conversion of T4 to T3 in response to different levothyroxine injections. Moreover, the optimal time between dosage adjustment should be identified. In relation to the possible factors that alter hormone levels, population studies should be carried out in order to characterize subgroups. Finally, a more detailed list of beneficiaries was defined. All of these considerations were used to define the future implementation of Hormonic.

Vanesa Daza


Member of the Wireless Communication Group within the department of Information and Communication Technology at the UPF. We contacted her with the aim of adapting the possible future implementation so that the wireless transmission of health related data is secure.

Input: she recommended the implementation of a symmetric-key algorithm, which makes use of cryptographic keys for the encryption of the incoming and outgoing data (concentration of T3 hormone and subsequent activation signal of the levothyroxine injection pumps) and the decryption of that same information (for PDI control computation and pump activation). In particular, she emphasized the high compatibility and good performance of the Advanced Encryption Standards (AES) 256. Furthermore, she suggested implementing the cryptography processes within the medical device, so that the patient does not have direct access to its key and the data transmission has one less open door. However, the corresponding endocrinologist should receive its own key from its department. In order to prevent external agents from injecting data, she suggested the use of digital signatures, at cost of a higher computational load.

Output: security aspects were gathered and summarized into the possible future implementation plan.

Clinical Research Ethic Committee - IMIM


the committee on the ethics of medicines research ensures ethical and legal correctness of any investigation project dealing with physical or psychological risk to a human being. It is managed by IMIM, the Hospital del Mar Institute for Medical Research. We contacted them to know about bioethics and biosafety issues related to the future implementation of Hormonic device.

Input: When it comes to the process of approval, the committee informed us that the first step is to accomplish a clinical trial on humans after obtaining the approval from the ethics committee itself and the AEMPS (Spanish agency for medicines and medical devices). Further steps would imply achieving the CE mark.

Output: Bioethical aspects were gathered and summarized into the possible future implementation plan.
